# cubism

Fractured, crystalline planes depicting subjects from multiple simultaneous viewpoints — a coffee grinder seen from the front, side, and above all at once, its form shattered into dozens of angular facets like a broken mirror reassembled by a mathematician. Muted earth tones of ochre, raw umber, and slate gray with sharp geometric edges colliding and overlapping, creating spatial ambiguity where foreground and background merge into a single flattened, fragmented surface. This is Picasso's Ma Jolie, Braque's Violin and Candlestick, Gris's Breakfast — reality deconstructed into interlocking geometric shards on parchment-toned grounds, every object analyzed from every angle simultaneously.

## Color Palette

- Primary: Raw umber (#826644), burnt sienna (#A0522D), ochre yellow (#C4A35A), slate gray (#708090)
- Background: Warm parchment (#E8DCC4), pale sand (#D4C4A8), muted olive (#7D7A5F), cool stone (#B8AFA0)
- Darks: Charcoal brown (#3D3226), deep olive (#4A4A3A), near-black umber (#2A231C), coffee (#3B2F2F)
- Accents: Dusty terracotta (#C4715B), muted teal-gray (#5F7A7D), faded blue-gray (#7A8B99), pale rose (#C9A9A9), dirty cream (#E8E0D0)
- Text: Deep brown (#3D3226) or charcoal on parchment grounds; cream (#E8DCC4) reversed out of dark faceted panels

### Palette Combinations

| Combination | Colors |
|-------------|--------|
| Analytical Cubism (1910-12) | Raw umber + slate gray + ochre + charcoal brown on parchment — the classic Braque/Picasso palette, near-monochromatic, all planes merging into tonal fog |
| Synthetic Cubism (1912-14) | Ochre + dusty terracotta + muted teal + faded blue-gray + cream — slightly more color variety, flatter planes, collage-like layering of distinct shapes |
| Juan Gris Elegance | Slate gray + pale rose + muted olive + burnt sienna on warm parchment — Gris's more decorative, structured approach with cleaner color separation |
| High Contrast | Near-black umber + ochre yellow + dirty cream — dramatic dark-light fragmentation with minimal color, maximum tonal drama between facets |

## Variants

| Variant | Focus | Visual Emphasis |
|---------|-------|-----------------|
| **Analytical** | Near-monochromatic shattered facets, deep spatial ambiguity | Dozens of small angular planes in barely-different earth tones — umber, gray, ochre — differences so subtle that forms dissolve and re-form. The most challenging variant: subjects almost unrecognizable beneath dense fragmentation |
| **Synthetic** | Flatter collage-like planes, textured fills, more distinct shapes | Larger geometric areas with cleaner edges, sections filled with faux wood grain, newsprint text, or stippled patterns. Overlapping flat planes like cut paper. Brighter, more legible than Analytical |
| **Gris Still Life** | Architectural structure, decorative precision | Juan Gris's more organized approach — subjects fragmented but carefully composed, each facet cleanly defined, recognizable objects (guitars, bottles, fruit) maintaining their identity while being geometrically restructured |
| **Orphist Color** | Delaunay-influenced prismatic color within cubist structure | Bolder color appearing within the faceted planes — dusty terracotta, teal, rose — still muted but with wider chromatic range. Circular forms and more rhythmic fragmentation |

## Visual Elements

- **Fragmented angular planes** — subjects shattered into dozens of interlocking triangular, trapezoidal, and irregular polygonal facets, each plane a slightly different tone of earth color. This is the single most defining Cubist visual trait
- **Simultaneous multiple viewpoints** — the same object rendered as if seen from front, side, top, and three-quarter view all compressed onto a single flat surface — a cup that is both round (seen from above) and cylindrical (seen from the side) at once
- **Overlapping transparent planes** — geometric facets that appear to pass through each other, creating ambiguous depth where solid forms interpenetrate like ghosted x-rays
- **Subtle tonal gradation within facets** — each plane gently shaded from light to dark, but the shading direction contradicts adjacent planes, deliberately confusing spatial logic
- **Sharp angular edges meeting at vertices** — no soft curves, everything reduced to straight lines intersecting at precise angles, even organic forms like fruit or faces become crystalline
- **Newspaper/text fragments as collage** — stenciled or painted letters (LE JOU, BAL, MA JOLIE), sheet music staves, newspaper column fragments scattered among the faceted planes
- **Faux texture fills** — trompe l'oeil wood graining painted with a comb, stippled dot patterns, striped hatching, crosshatch shading within individual facets
- **Thick dark edge lines on select boundaries** — some facet edges emphasized with heavy brown or charcoal lines while others dissolve softly into adjacent planes, creating a rhythm of definition and ambiguity
- **Passages** — deliberate gaps where plane edges open into each other, forms left unfinished so the eye flows through rather than stopping at a boundary
- **Shallow compressed picture space** — no deep perspective recession, everything pushed toward the picture plane into a bas-relief depth of only inches
- **Still life objects as recurring motifs** — guitars, violins, wine glasses, bottles, fruit bowls, pipes, newspapers, playing cards — the classic cubist subject vocabulary
- **Stenciled numerals and letters** — crisp typographic forms floating among fractured planes, industrial and impersonal against the hand-painted facets

## Compositional Patterns

| Content Structure | Composition | Reference |
|-------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Hierarchy/levels | Central dominant subject fractured into the largest, most defined facets, with peripheral content shattered into progressively smaller, denser fragments that dissolve at the edges | Picasso's Portrait of Ambroise Vollard — face assembled from central facets, background disintegrating |
| Flow/process | Diagonal cascade of overlapping faceted planes moving from upper-left to lower-right, each stage a new layer of fragmentation through which the subject transforms | Duchamp's Nude Descending a Staircase — sequential motion compressed into simultaneous facets |
| Connections | Shared facet boundaries where one form's edge becomes another's start — relationships expressed through abutting planes and overlapping areas | Braque's violin paintings — instrument parts connected through adjacent shared edges |
| Cycles | Circular arrangement of faceted forms rotating around a central point, the same subject viewed from progressive angles as you follow the circle | A wine glass seen from every angle arranged in a radial pattern |
| Comparison | Split composition with the same subject fragmented in two different ways — Analytical (dense, monochromatic) on one side, Synthetic (flat, colorful) on the other | Before-and-after of cubist deconstruction |
| Categories | Different recognizable objects arranged in a still life grouping, each object fragmented independently but sharing the same tonal ground | Gris's tabletop with guitar, bottle, newspaper, glass |
| Growth | Progressive fragmentation from an intact recognizable form at one edge to fully shattered abstract planes at the other — the act of deconstruction made visible | A whole object gradually shattering across the canvas |
| Networks | Multiple fragmented subject nodes sharing edges and overlapping planes, the negative space between them equally fragmented, creating a unified field of angular planes | Web of interlocking forms where figure and ground are equally shattered |
| Timeline | Horizontal frieze of increasingly fragmented forms, time represented as progressive deconstruction of stable recognizable form into abstract facets | Chronological dissolution from realism to abstraction |
| Parts-to-whole | Large recognizable form emerging from dozens of smaller overlapping angular shards — step back and see the whole, step close and see the parts | Portrait emerging from mosaic of tonal facets |

## Visual Metaphor Mappings

| Abstract Concept | Cubism Metaphor |
|------------------|-----------------|
| Data/information | Fragmented planes of proportional size — larger facets for major data points, tiny dense facets for fine-grained details, all interlocking on a shared ground |
| Processes | Angular faceted path cutting through the composition, each stage a different fragment angle on the subject, connected by shared edges |
| Systems | Interlocking planes that share boundaries — remove one facet and the adjacent ones lose their context, the whole emerges from precisely fitted geometric shards |
| Connections | Shared facet edges where one form's boundary IS another form's boundary — overlap zones where planes interpenetrate |
| Growth | Progressive fragmentation: an intact form at one end shattering into increasing numbers of facets at the other, complexity increasing |
| Time | Multiple moments collapsed into a single image — the same subject seen from sequential viewpoints simultaneously, duration compressed into one frame |
| Hierarchy | Larger, more clearly defined planes for important content; smaller, denser, more ambiguous fragmentation for secondary information |
| Protection | Enclosing angular boundary of heavy-edged faceted planes, content held inside the shattered frame, darker tones forming a border |
| Speed | Diagonal thrust of angular planes cascading in one direction, sharp wedge shapes pointing forward, motion through fragmented space |
| Stability | Large anchoring faceted form at composition center or base, smaller fragments radiating from the stable core |
| Users/people | Faces and figures constructed from angular facets — eyes at different angles, profiles merged with frontal views, features scattered across geometric planes yet still recognizable as human |
| Ideas | Light-toned facets radiating from a bright center point, illumination fragmenting outward through the plane structure |
| Energy | Dense clustering of small sharp angular facets, aggressive collision of opposing plane directions, compressed spatial tension |
| Tension/conflict | Opposing facet directions clashing at a jagged diagonal boundary — warm umber tones vs. cool gray-blue tones in adjacent warring planes |
| Complexity | Denser fragmentation with more facets per area — simple concepts get fewer, larger planes; complex concepts become dense mosaics of tiny shards |

## Typography

- Angular geometric sans-serif letterforms that echo the faceted plane structure — letters that feel constructed from straight edges
- Letters fragmenting across plane boundaries — a word split by a facet edge, the two halves at slightly different angles or tonal values
- Stenciled industrial letterforms painted into the composition, like those Braque and Picasso incorporated directly onto their canvases — LE JOURNAL, BACH, MA JOLIE
- Text as visual element rather than pure communication — newspaper column fragments, isolated letters, partial words contributing to visual texture
- Titles placed within angular faceted boxes that continue the cubist fragmentation of the surrounding composition
- Muted brown, charcoal, or ochre text colors harmonizing with the earth-tone palette — text should not pop out of the surface but integrate into it
- Headlines can split across multiple planes at different angles, readable but requiring the eye to reassemble them
- NO decorative scripts, NO rounded friendly faces, NO playful styling — typography must feel angular, constructed, and architectonic

## Anti-Patterns

- **NOT abstract geometric art (Kandinsky, Mondrian)** — Cubism depicts RECOGNIZABLE SUBJECTS (faces, guitars, bottles, figures) that have been fragmented. If you cannot identify any real-world subject within the facets, it is not Cubism — it is abstract art. The subject must be present, just shattered.
- **NOT a scattered collage of random shapes** — cubist fragments must FIT TOGETHER with precision, sharing edges, creating a unified but shattered whole. Random floating triangles on a blank background are NOT Cubism. Every facet relates to its neighbors.
- **NOT bright or saturated colors** — Cubism uses MUTED EARTH TONES, near-monochromatic palettes of umber, ochre, gray, and brown. Hot pink, electric blue, or vivid primaries belong to other movements. If the colors are exciting, it is wrong.
- **NOT Memphis Design** — Memphis uses thick outlines, flat neon fills, zigzag patterns, and deliberate clash. Cubism uses subtle tonal gradation, earth tones, and spatial ambiguity. They are polar opposites despite both using geometric shapes.
- **NOT Bauhaus or De Stijl** — those movements use pure geometric forms (circles, squares, rectangles) in primary colors as abstract compositions. Cubism FRAGMENTS REAL OBJECTS into angular planes. A red square is Mondrian. A guitar shattered into umber facets is Cubism.
- **NOT clean flat design** — Cubism has TONAL GRADATION within each facet, visible texture (wood grain, newsprint), and spatial ambiguity. Perfectly flat solid color fills are too simple and too clean.
- **NOT symmetrical or orderly** — cubist fragmentation should feel complex, dense, and spatially disorienting. If the composition looks tidy and organized, it needs more shatter.
- **NOT deep perspective space** — everything must feel compressed toward the picture plane, shallow and relief-like. If forms recede convincingly into deep space, the spatial compression has failed.
- This must look like a 1910-1912 Picasso or Braque canvas — muted earth tones, angular interlocking facets, recognizable subjects shattered into geometric planes, spatial ambiguity, newspaper fragments, shallow compressed space — not a modern geometric design exercise that happens to use triangles.
