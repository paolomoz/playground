# googie

Swooping rooflines reaching toward the stars like frozen seagull wings, boomerang-shaped canopies cantilevered impossibly over plate glass windows, neon signs blazing hot coral and turquoise against deepening twilight skies — the jet-age optimism of 1950s American roadside architecture where every coffee shop looks ready to launch into low Earth orbit. Angled pylons tilt at dramatic angles supporting signs that can be read at 60mph, starburst decorations explode like atomic fireworks on every flat surface, and chrome trim gleams under buzzing neon. This is Bob's Big Boy at sunset, the Theme Building at LAX, Norm's on La Cienega Boulevard glowing against a purple sky — Space Age confidence made concrete and steel, the American highway promising that the future is just one more exit ahead, and it serves great pie.

## Visual Elements

- **Swept rooflines and canopies** — dramatic upward-curving roof shapes with sharp points at their extended tips, like a seagull's wings frozen mid-sweep or a flying saucer's edge about to take off. The roofline IS Googie — it's the element that turns a building into a spaceship. Always asymmetric, always dynamic
- **Angled pylons and support columns** — structural supports tilted at dramatic 15-30 degree angles away from vertical, tapering to points at their base, painted in contrasting colors. Buildings that LEAN into the future, their support structure making a visual argument against gravity
- **Boomerang and parabola shapes** — asymmetric curved forms used everywhere: sign shapes, counter profiles, decorative wall elements, table edges. The boomerang is to Googie what the column is to classical architecture — the fundamental formal vocabulary
- **Starbursts and atomic asterisks** — multi-pointed star shapes with 8-12 elongated points decorating everything from wall clocks to sign tops to pattern accents. The visual signature of the Atomic Age, each starburst suggesting a tiny controlled nuclear reaction of optimism
- **Neon tube signage** — glowing tubular letterforms and arrow shapes rendered with visible color-bleeding halos around the tubes against dark backgrounds. The glow extends 5-10px beyond the tube edge. Colors are hottest (brightest) at the tube center, fading through the halo
- **Massive plate glass windows** — large angular window walls divided by thin aluminum mullions into geometric patterns (triangles, parallelograms, not rectangles). Interior and exterior flowing together, the plate glass erasing the boundary between inside and outside
- **Chrome and polished metal surfaces** — reflective silver trim on structural edges, stool pedestals, counter lips, sign borders. Rendered with a sharp highlight line and subtle tonal gradation suggesting curved polished metal. The machine-age sheen promising a gleaming future
- **Kidney and amoeba shapes** — organic freeform blob silhouettes for swimming pools seen from above, planter beds, coffee table surfaces, decorative wall cutouts. Biomorphic curves as counterpoint to the sharp angular architecture
- **Zigzag and chevron patterns** — angular decorative borders, folded-plate ceiling textures, zigzag rooflines on secondary structures. Serrated energy lines suggesting vibration and forward motion
- **Flying saucer and orbital motifs** — disc shapes (the Theme Building form), Saturn-ring decorative details, antenna spires reaching upward from rooftops. Space Age iconography made permanent in concrete and steel
- **Bold directional arrow shapes** — oversized arrow pointers integrated into signs and architectural elements, promising speed, direction, forward motion, and a destination worth arriving at
- **Terrazzo and aggregate surface textures** — speckled floor and counter surfaces with embedded chips of color (turquoise, coral, gold) in neutral cream or gray ground — Space Age materials visible underfoot
- **NO scattered floating decorative elements** — everything is ANCHORED to architectural structure. Starbursts are ON buildings, arrows are PART of signs, shapes are ATTACHED to pylons. Nothing floats randomly in space

## Typography

- **Bold geometric sans-serif with exaggerated proportions** — letters stretched wide, angled to match swept rooflines, constructed with the same confidence as the architecture. Titles that LEAN forward
- **Neon tube lettering** — letterforms rendered as if bent from glass tubes, with characteristic tube-corner rounding where strokes change direction, visible neon glow halos in the letter's color bleeding into the dark background. The letters themselves are light sources
- **Titles at dramatic angles** following the sweep of a roofline or pylon (10-20 degree tilts off horizontal), never sitting flat and passive
- **Mixed case** for approachability and friendliness — but KEY WORDS in ALL CAPS for signage impact and highway readability
- **Script and brush lettering** for accent words — the "Coffee Shop" in cursive neon beneath the "BOB'S" in block letters. Warm, human, inviting beneath the bold signage
- **Chrome-effect or dimensional lettering** for feature headlines — letters that pop forward with a sharp highlight along their top edge and a shadow beneath, suggesting polished metal
- **NO serif fonts, NO blackletter, NO condensed modern grotesks** — this is bold, confident, generous mid-century signage lettering that WANTS to be read from a speeding car
- Number forms large, round, friendly, and confident — speedometer-readable, diner-menu-friendly

## Anti-Patterns

- **NOT Art Deco** — Art Deco is formal, vertical, symmetrical, ornamental with gold and geometric luxury. Googie is optimistic, HORIZONTAL, asymmetrical, and populist. No ziggurats, no gold leaf, no symmetrical fountain shapes. Googie swoops dynamically; Deco steps elegantly. They share an era's distance but not a single design impulse
- **NOT generic atomic-age or mid-century modern** — while related through the 1950s, Googie is specifically ARCHITECTURAL and ROADSIDE. The building silhouette and the neon sign must dominate. A starburst on a cream background is mid-century decoration. A starburst mounted on a swept canopy over a plate glass window next to an angled pylon sign — THAT is Googie
- **NOT Memphis Design** — Memphis is deliberately ugly 1980s postmodernism with terrazzo, zigzags, and clashing patterns. Googie is sincere 1950s optimism with chrome, swept curves, and genuine belief in the future. Memphis is ironic; Googie is earnest. They don't share a universe
- **NOT realistic vintage photography or sepia nostalgia** — this is STYLIZED ILLUSTRATION of Googie architecture, not a filtered Instagram photo. Bold, saturated colors, clean illustrated shapes, strong compositions. Rendering Googie as nostalgic sepia defeats its entire forward-looking ideology
- **NOT Vegas-kitschy or ironic camp** — Googie has GENUINE optimism and structural confidence. It is not a joke. Avoid tongue-in-cheek, retro-ironic, or campy treatments — play it straight, with respect for the architecture's sincere belief in progress
- **NOT corporate flat illustration with mid-century colors** — this needs ACTUAL ARCHITECTURAL FORM. Perspective, structural shadow, material texture. The building is the star, not generic icons on a turquoise background. If there's no building silhouette, there's no Googie
- **NOT Futurism** — Futurism is aggressive, violent, diagonal, chaotic. Googie is confident, welcoming, sweeping, optimistic. Futurism worships speed; Googie worships the destination. One is a crashing train; the other is the diner at the end of the highway
- **NO scattered floating elements** — elements must be ANCHORED to architectural structures (pylons, canopies, sign posts, rooflines). Nothing floats randomly in space. Googie is ARCHITECTURE, and architecture is attached to the ground
